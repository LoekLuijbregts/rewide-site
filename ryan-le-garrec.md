## About

Ryan Le Garrec grew up wanting to be a war reporter. His father was one — dodging bullets to document what the world would rather not see. That instinct to be present at difficult things, to find the honest image in uncomfortable places, shaped everything that followed.

He went to cinema school in Belgium. He shot music videos for Damien Rice. He co-directed a television show about the people of Brussels. He worked across Europe for fifteen years before cycling found him — or rather, before he found cycling.

He became a bike messenger in Brussels. Then a rider. Then a filmmaker who happened to be on a bicycle. The sequence matters: he is not a photographer who cycles, he is a cyclist who cannot stop documenting.

His work covers the extreme edge of the sport: ultra-endurance races, multi-week bikepacking expeditions, the kind of cycling that most people know nothing about. He has followed Tour Divide riders through the American West, documented the Race Around Rwanda, and ridden Morocco's mountain roads on a road bike with 25mm tyres simply because it seemed right.

He has been published in Rouleur — the finest cycling magazine in the world, by most accounts — and featured on Bikepacking.com, GCN, and in The Bikepacking Journal. His films are studied by other filmmakers as examples of a particular kind of intimacy: the camera close enough to hear breathing, the edit unhurried enough to let silence happen.

His photography shares those qualities. His best images are accidental in the best possible sense — the wrong exposure, the blur of motion, the frame that any editor would cut but that contains something a clean image never could.

> *"I try to fail on purpose. I don't believe my eyes are as good as the accidents that can happen. My best shots are accidental in photography, and my favourite shots in video are usually what any editor would cut away."*

---

## On his work

Ryan defines himself as a multi-media artist, wannabe vagabond cyclist, and a keen credit-card bikepacker. The self-deprecation is part of the honesty. He is also one of the most distinctive visual voices in cycling media today.

His images from Morocco — made during a solo ride across the Atlas Mountains — generated significant attention when published on Bikepacking.com and simultaneously in Rouleur. The same trip became a film. The same film became the subject of discussion about what cycling documentary can be when it refuses to be inspirational in a conventional sense.
